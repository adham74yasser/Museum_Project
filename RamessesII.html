RAMSES II

The Great Pharaoh of the Nineteenth Dynasty

Name : Ramesses II

Dynasty : The 19th Dynasty

Reign Duration : 66 to 67 years

Year of Death : 1213 BC.

Age at Death :90 to 91 old

Ramesses II: The Great Pharaoh

Ramesses II, also known as "Ramesses the Great," was the third king of the Nineteenth Dynasty (1304–1195 BC) and remains one of the most prominent and influential figures in Ancient Egyptian history. His legacy extended beyond massive constructions and battles to include administrative governance, diplomatic policy, and royal propaganda that established the concept of "eternity" in the New Kingdom. Ramesses II took the throne around 1279 BC following the death of his father, Seti I. His ascension was organized and expected, as Seti I had chosen him as crown prince and involved him in state affairs, ensuring a smooth transition of power. It is estimated that Ramesses was no older than 25 when he took office, and inscriptions indicate he was already a father to two children whom he took on campaigns to Nubia. The most notable feature of his reign was its exceptional length, spanning approximately 66 years (1279 BC – 1213 BC), and he passed away at nearly 90 years of age. This long lifespan acted as an unprecedented strategic factor, allowing him to outlast a generation of rivals, including the rulers of the Hittite Empire, and avoid early succession crises. This stability enabled him to execute long-term military and engineering plans, reinforcing the public perception of his "eternal" rule linked to the continuity of the state.Administratively, the King remained the center of power, representing divine authority and the necessary mediator between gods and humans. His massive military and construction projects were made possible by an efficient administrative system and a strong bureaucracy. His administration was characterized by efficiency in tax collection, developing a system for both in-kind and monetary taxes collected primarily through agricultural crops, local goods, minerals, and direct contributions from the population. This bureaucratic efficiency ensured the continuous funding required for his enormous building operations. A major geopolitical shift occurred when he established a new capital, Pi-Ramesses, in the Delta region. This move placed the military and administrative center closer to the front lines, allowing for a faster response to threats from Asia, such as the Hittites, and Libyan military threats in the Delta. Pi-Ramesses became a thriving center of political, cultural, and religious activity, adorned with temples, palaces, and giant statues of the King.Ramesses II was also exceptionally keen on royal propaganda to promote his name and battle stories throughout Egypt and Nubia. He practiced "monument usurpation" (Usurpation), deeply carving his cartouches into the stones and statues of previous kings to integrate his legacy into the state's older historical fabric, presenting himself as the legitimate and eternal heir to all architectural achievements. His foreign policy was a complex mix of military intervention and realism, centered on the struggle for influence in the Levant against the Hittite Empire. The most prominent clash was the Second Battle of Kadesh around 1274 BC. During this battle, Ramesses fell into an intelligence trap set by Shasu spies who convinced him the Hittite army was far away in Aleppo. Consequently, he rushed ahead with the Amun division, leaving large gaps between his divisions. The Hittite army, hidden behind Kadesh with 50,000 soldiers and 3,700 chariots, launched a surprise attack that destroyed the Ra division and isolated Ramesses. Ramesses personally led a counter-attack, and the arrival of unexpected reinforcements from "Ne’arin" forces forced the Hittites to retreat. While the battle was a tactical victory because he survived, it was strategically inconclusive as Kadesh remained under Hittite control. Despite this, his propaganda turned the near-defeat into a divine personal victory to boost his domestic popularity. Eventually, military exhaustion led both sides to sign the Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty around 1259 BC, the oldest recorded peace treaty in history.His architectural achievements covered the land from the Delta to Nubia. These projects aimed to deify the King and confirm sovereignty over the territories. The Abu Simbel temples, carved into rock near the Second Cataract, are prime examples of this "engineering of power". The Great Temple was dedicated to deifying Ramesses himself, while the Small Temple was dedicated to his primary wife, Nefertari. In Luxor, the Ramesseum served as his main mortuary temple for his worship after death. Personally, Ramesses had a massive royal family with an estimated 90 children to ensure the continuation of the royal line. Nefertari was his most important wife, and her high status was a political tool to promote the image of the "divine family". Due to his long life, many of his eldest sons died before him, and he was eventually succeeded by his thirteenth son, Merneptah. Studies of his mummy revealed that in his final years, he suffered from tooth decay, abscesses, and possibly dementia (memory loss). He was buried in tomb KV7 in the Valley of the Kings, but his mummy was later moved to the Deir el-Bahari cache (DB320) to protect it from grave robbers. Historical evaluation agrees that he was a brilliant military leader and an efficient administrator whose era witnessed wide prosperity. Religious debate also persists regarding whether he was the "Pharaoh of the Exodus," a point that remains unresolved by modern archaeological studies but highlights the importance of his reign as a pivotal meeting point for ancient Near Eastern civilizations.
